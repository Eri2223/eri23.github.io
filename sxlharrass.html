What is Sexual Harassment?

Sexual Harassment Description:

Sexual harassment is any unwelcome sexual behavior, whether verbal, nonverbal, or physical, that violates someone's dignity is prohibited, especially when it results in an intimidating, hostile, demeaning, humiliating, or insulting atmosphere.

Sexual harassment includes unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, and other verbal or physical harassment of a sexual nature in the workplace or learning environment

Signs that you may be a victim of Sexual Harassment:

1. You feel uncomfortable

Many sexual harassment victims are unaware that they have likely been harassed if a supervisor or coworker's actions make them uncomfortable because of their sex. Maintaining or tolerating sexualized behavior, language, or environments that are so pervasive they have a negative impact on an employee's work—or make it impossible to work—is considered sexual harassment.

2. Insulted

Insults are fundamentally degrading and are meant to make the target feel inferior. Sexually charged insults are a more aggressive sort of name-calling that can't be covered up with "I was only kidding" statements, whether they are commonplace or truly surprising.

3. You are being treated differently based on your sex

When someone harasses another person because of that person's sex, gender identity, or sexual orientation, they are breaking the law. Examples of this include the person who constantly stands too close to you in the elevator and the person who blocks your path whenever you walk down the corridor.

4. Coercion

Quid pro quo sexual harassment occurs when a person in a position of authority refuses to grant an employee a benefit, such as a pay increase, promotion, job assignment, or approval of earned vacation days, unless the worker engages in a sexual act. No one should believe that doing sex activities is necessary to keep or advance in their position.

5. Sexual gestures

There are inappropriate venues for sexual gestures. Sexual harassment includes making gestures or movements that make employees feel uneasy because of their sex, regardless of who the actions are directed towards, in addition to being impolite and disrespectful.

6. Sexualized conversation or lewd communication

When someone often uses sexualized language to describe their profession or spends time freely discussing their sex lives, sexual acts, pornography, or other sexual topics, they are acting in a harassing manner.

7. You are being subjected to jokes or images of sexual nature

A company that accepts crude jokes, whether they are spoken, written, or "practical", is condoning sexual harassment. Sexual harassment includes jokes that make fun of someone because of their sex, gender identity, or sexual orientation. Employees are free to file a complaint about sexual harassment through their employer's HR department or through other channels.

8. Saying “no” has no impact

Although it is not ideal, dating someone at work is not inherently criminal and is not legally against the law either. However, when someone advances someone at a place without their consent, the law does get involved. If a friend has expressed romantic interest in you but you have rejected them, they may still be engaging in sexual harassment if you feel uncomfortable with this.

9. You feel unsafe

A clear sign that you are a victim of sexual harassment is feeling uneasy in a sexually charged situation.

What do you need to do when you get harassed:

Look for people who can help, approach someone, or go into a crowde area to lessen the chances of the situation to happen.

If you believe that you are going to suffer harm soon, call the police right away. Call the police if you are unsure. Call the police to have them enforce your restraining order. Police can detain your harasser for the harassment as well as any other laws they may have broken.
